World Cup Increases NYC Flight Traffic by Less Than 2%

The biggest World Cup ever did not translate into a proportional rise in air traffic

By Marcela Villar | 14 June 2026

The 2026 World Cup began with the promise of becoming the biggest edition of all times. For the first time in the tournament’s history, a record number of teams are competing for the trophy after FIFA expanded the number of participating countries to 48 — up from 32 since 1998. The expansion affected air traffic, but not at the same rate.

Considering only flights arriving in New York City — home to the tournament’s largest stadium and the venue for the final — there was a modest increase of less than 2% in the number of flights compared with the same period last year. In 2025, the United States hosted another major soccer event: the FIFA Club World Cup, held from June 14 to July 13.

The period analyzed this year ran from June 6, one week before the start of the championship, through the first three weeks of matches, ending on July 5. This was the date of the last match hosted by New York, when Brazil was eliminated by Norway after a 2–1 defeat.

For the analysis, we considered flights arriving at John F. Kennedy International Airport (JFK), LaGuardia Airport (LGA), and Newark Liberty International Airport (EWR). The data was collected from OpenSky, a network founded in 2012 by European researchers to provide open access to air traffic data.

On the other hand, nine of the ten busiest days at New York-area airports occurred within a four-day window before a World Cup match.

Where did they come from?

Not from very far away. Most flights came from within the United States. The largest number of arrivals came from airports in Chicago, Atlanta, Boston, Los Angeles, and Orlando. Among international flights, the highest number of departures came from Canada, England, France, and Italy.

The Biggest World Cup Ever?

The expansion in the number of participating countries increased the number of matches from 64 to 104, while also extending the tournament from 28 to 39 days — making it the longest World Cup ever. It was also the first time FIFA adopted a three-country hosting model, with the United States, Mexico, and Canada sharing hosting duties and matches spread across 16 cities.

The largest stadium is the New York New Jersey Stadium, better known as MetLife Stadium, with a capacity of more than 78,000 fans. Alongside Los Angeles, Miami, and Atlanta, it will host the highest number of matches — eight in total. Dallas will host the most matches, with nine.

According to FIFA, the expansion of participating teams was designed to allow countries that had never competed in the tournament to take part in the World Cup. This year, four national teams made their debut: Cape Verde, Curaçao, Jordan, and Uzbekistan. FIFA is already considering expanding the tournament to 64 teams in 2030, marking the competition’s centennial anniversary.
